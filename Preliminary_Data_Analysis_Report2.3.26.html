  1. Business Proposal

1.1 Business Case This study addresses a critical operational challenge of rising crime incidents for public safety agencies. By analyzing the crime‑incident dataset, the organization can identify patterns in offense types, hotspots, response times, and repeat‑offender dynamics insights that directly influence staffing efficiency, resource allocation, and prevention strategies. The value proposition lies in transforming raw incident data into actionable intelligence that improves patrol deployment and enhances community safety outcomes. A focused, data‑driven assessment ensures leadership can prioritize interventions that deliver the highest return in safety, efficiency, and community trust.

1.2 Business Question Which wards, BIDs, and census tracts should be prioritized for targeted policing, environmental design (lighting, cameras), and social programs based on 2020–2026 crime patterns by offense type and weapon use?

At what times (season, month, day of week, shift) and for which crime types should MPD increase or adjust deployment to achieve the largest reduction in violent and property crime?

1.3 Analytics Question

How do temporal features (month, day of week, shift) and location characteristics (ward, BID, tract) jointly predict the likelihood and type of incident, and can these patterns inform scenario‑based deployment recommendations?

  1. Description of Dataset

The dataset is comprised of Crime Incidents from 2020 - 2026 reported by District of Columbia Metropolitan Police Department.The crime data was published on Open Data DC by the DC Office of the Chief Technology Officer (OCTO) and the Metropolitan Police Department (MPD).

The 2025 dataset can be found at think link: https://opendata.dc.gov/datasets/DCGIS::crime-incidents-in-2025/about. To view other years just update the year in the link; for example, 2024’s dataset link would be https://opendata.dc.gov/datasets/DCGIS::crime-incidents-in-2024/about.

The key variables we are using for prediction are the ward in which the crime was reported, the type of crime, and the month of the crime. We are condensing the multitude of crime types listed into four groups: homicide, robbery/burglary, theft, and sex abuse/arson/assault. Other interesting variables not included in this baseline model are shift, method, and neighborhood.

  1. Initial Set of Predictors

The main variables we are seeking to use are WARD, a simplified version of the offense and date column, and a count of the total crime occurrences in each of those groups. Because we are seeking to help the city of DC determine where best to send resources for crime prevention, this breakdown should allow us to give the city some insights on where and what types of crimes are happening and potentially guide them toward finding ways to stop it.

  1. OLS Assumption Testing

We assessed the standard OLS assumptions using diagnostic plots and formal tests on the linear model of total monthly crime counts by ward and crime type. The residuals vs fitted and scale–location plots show a clear increase in the spread of residuals at higher fitted values, which is confirmed by the Breusch–Pagan test (BP = 519, df = 11, p‑value < 2e‑16); together these indicate strong heteroskedasticity and a violation of the constant‑variance assumption. The Q–Q plot exhibits pronounced departures from the reference line in both tails, implying that residuals are not normally distributed, which is expected given that the outcome is a skewed count variable.

To evaluate independence, we used the Durbin–Watson test, which reported a Durbin–Watson statistic of approximately 1.51 with a p‑value near zero, indicating significant positive autocorrelation in the residuals and thus a violation of the independence assumption over time. In contrast, multicollinearity does not appear to be a concern: generalized VIF values for month, crime_type, and WARD are all close to 1, suggesting that these predictors are not excessively correlated with one another. The residuals vs leverage plot shows some moderately influential observations but no extreme leverage points that dominate the model fit. Overall, the main OLS assumption issues arise from heteroskedasticity, non‑normal residuals, and autocorrelation, which likely stem from modeling discrete crime counts over time with a simple linear regression.

  1. Conclusion

The baseline model explains a large section of variation in monthly crime counts by ward and crime type, with an adjusted R squared of 0.845, but several OLS assumptions are violated. The outcome, count of crimes, shows strong heteroskedasticity and positive autocorrelation. This indicates that simple linear regression is not an appropriate modeling framework for this data set.

Going forward, we should consider modeling the log of crime counts to improve normality, using a GLM model for prediction, or explicitly accounting for time dependence by adding lagged crime variables. At the same time, the low VIFs and lack of outliers indicates that ward, month, and crime type are stable predictors, so we should continue to work with them in future models.
